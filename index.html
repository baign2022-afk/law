    🔻 Ways to Reduce Your Cost

    🎯Earn a merit scholarship
    • A 170+ LSAT can unlock full-tuition at T25 schools — worth $150K+
    • Apply where your LSAT is above the 75th percentile median
    • Always negotiate after receiving an offer — 70%+ of schools will increase
    • Check whether scholarship has GPA retention requirement (usually 3.0+)
    💼Work part-time during school
    • Most law schools allow up to 20 hrs/week work (ABA limit)
    • RA positions, law review, tutoring: $15–$25/hr
    • Summer associate programs pay $3,500–$4,500/week BigLaw
    • Clerking for a judge during school can pay stipends
    🏛️Public service path (LRAP/PSLF)
    • PSLF forgives ALL remaining federal loans after 10 years of public service
    • Most T14 schools have LRAP that covers loan payments for public interest jobs
    • IBR/SAVE plans cap monthly payments at 5–10% of discretionary income
    • Government/non-profit = PSLF eligible from day 1 after bar admission
    💡Strategic school choice
    • A full ride at Emory (#18) vs $300K debt at Columbia (#4) — run the ROI math
    • In-state public schools: University of Texas, UNC, UGA — exceptional value
    • Regional firms often hire heavily from local schools at lower rates
    • Military JAG: law school debt paid + $80K+ salary on Day 1
    ✅ The best-case scenario
    Score 170+ → get full ride at a T20 school ($0 tuition × 3 years) → work as a summer associate ($30–$40K in 2L summer) → graduate with under $60K in living-expense debt → BigLaw salary of $225K/yr pays that off in 6–8 months. This is achievable and many students do it every year.

    Law School Scholarship Guide
    How to fund your legal education and minimize debt
    📋Types of aid
    • Merit scholarships — LSAT + GPA. Most impactful lever you control.
    • Need-based grants — FAFSA-determined. Varies significantly by school.
    • Diversity scholarships — race, gender, first-gen, LGBTQ+, veterans.
    • Public interest fellowships — LRAP programs forgive loans for public roles.
    • External scholarships — private orgs, bar foundations, employers.
    • State bar scholarships — most state bars fund 1–3 students per year.
    🎯The LSAT leverage strategy
    • Every 3-point LSAT increase can unlock $15,000–$40,000/yr more in merit aid.
    • Apply to schools where your LSAT is above their 75th percentile.
    • A 170+ opens full-ride offers at T14–T25 schools regularly.
    • Retake if below your target school median — scholarship money follows the score.
    • Use 7Sage scholarship estimator to model your package before applying.
    💰Top external scholarships
    • LSAC Diversity Scholarship — up to $15,000 for underrepresented students.
    • Paul & Daisy Soros Fellowship — $90,000 for New Americans in any grad program.
    • ABA Legal Opportunity Scholarship — $15,000 for diverse 1Ls.
    • Hispanic National Bar Foundation — multiple awards for Latinx students.
    • National Black Law Students Assoc. — regional and national awards.
    • Women's Bar Association — state chapters fund $1,000–$10,000.
    📬Negotiating your offer
    • Always negotiate — 70%+ of schools will increase offers if asked professionally.
    • Use competing offers as leverage — name the school and the amount.
    • Write a 1-page letter: express genuine interest, state competing offer, ask for match.
    • Call your admissions contact — a 10-min call beats email every time.
    • Negotiate after deposit deadline — waitlisted spots free up scholarship funds.
    • Ask about scholarship retention GPA requirements (usually 3.0).
    ⚖️LRAP & PSLF explained
    • If public service is your path, LRAP can be worth more than any scholarship.
    • Yale, Harvard, Stanford, Columbia all offer LRAP covering loans fully for public interest.
    • Federal PSLF forgives remaining balance after 10 years of qualifying payments.
    • IBR/SAVE plans cap monthly payments at 5–10% of discretionary income.
    • Apply for school LRAP in your 3L year — most programs require annual renewal.
    📅Application timeline
    June–Aug: Take the LSAT
    Aim for your target score before October. Oct/Nov retakes are fine.
    Sep–Oct: Research schools
    Build your list: 3 reach, 4 target, 3 safety. Check median LSAT.
    Oct–Nov: Submit applications
    Apply early — rolling admissions = first-come, first-served for scholarships.
    Jan–Mar: Scholarship offers arrive
    Compare packages. Begin negotiation letters.
    Apr 15: Decision deadline
    Deposit deadline. Negotiate up until this date with competing offers.
    💡 The "scholarship splitter" strategy
    Apply to schools where your LSAT is 5+ points above their median. Schools ranked #15–#50 regularly offer full-tuition scholarships to attract high-LSAT students, even with lower GPAs. A 172 with a 3.4 GPA might be rejected from Yale but offered a full ride at Emory, Wake Forest, or Notre Dame. Run the math — a full ride at a T25 often beats $50K/yr debt at a T5.

    Scoring 170+ on the LSAT
    What the 97th+ percentile actually takes — and a complete 16-week study plan

    Understanding the score scale

    120–149Below 40th pct.
    Below most law school medians. Structured prep with a retake is strongly recommended.
    150–15940th–70th pct.
    Competitive for T3 and regional schools. Scholarships limited. Strong GPA can offset.
    160–164~80th pct.
    Strong. Competitive for T1 schools outside T14. Merit scholarships start here.
    165–169~90th–96th pct.
    Excellent. Competitive at T14. Strong merit scholarship territory at T15–T50.
    170–17497th–99th pct.
    Full scholarship territory at T14. Competitive at Yale/Harvard/Stanford. Life-changing for your options.
    175–18099th+ pct.
    ~500 people per year. Virtually guarantees admission and full scholarship at any US law school.

    What separates 170+ scorers

    🧠Mindset
    • Don't memorize tricks — understand the reasoning structure behind every question type.
    • Every wrong answer has a specific, identifiable flaw. Find it, don't skip it.
    • Drill weaknesses, not strengths. Comfort ≠ improvement.
    • Aim for -0 or -1 on Logic Games. LG is the most learnable section.
    • Read every answer choice — even when you "know" the answer.
    📚Best resources
    • LSAT Demon — AI-adaptive, built specifically for 170+ chasers.
    • 7Sage + J.Y. Ping explanations — video explanations for every LSAC question.
    • LSAC LawHub — 70+ released PrepTests. Do every one timed.
    • PowerScore Logic Games Bible — master diagramming first.
    • Blind Review method — timed then unlimited. Gap = your ceiling.
    ⏱️Test day timing
    • LR: 1 min 25 sec per question average. Skip and return — never sink on one Q.
    • LG: 1–2 min diagramming each game before solving saves time overall.
    • RC: Read for structure, not details. Mark where author argues and concedes.
    • Flag and return: mark any question taking 90+ seconds.
    • Never leave blanks — no penalty for wrong answers.
    📊Tracking progress
    • Keep an error log for every wrong answer: question type, why wrong, correct reasoning.
    • After each PT: categorize errors by question type.
    • Track Blind Review score. If BR - timed score > 5, issue is speed not understanding.
    • Max 1 PT per week early on, 2/week in final month.

    Tue
    LR: Study "Must Be True" and "Main Point" question types. Do 20 untimed LR questions. (2 hrs)
    Wed
    RC: Read 2 passages, practice identifying structure: main point, author's tone, evidence vs. claim. (1.5 hrs)
    Thu

    Fri

    Sat

    Sun
    Error review: For every wrong answer, write out WHY the correct answer is correct. (1.5 hrs)

    Mon
    LG: Complete all game types (sequencing, grouping, in/out, combo). 4 games untimed, then retimed. (2.5 hrs)
    Tue
    LR: Deep dive on Strengthen/Assumption/Sufficient Assumption — 35%+ of LR. Do 30 questions. (2.5 hrs)
    Wed
    RC: Comparative reading passages. Practice passage mapping. Time: 3.5 min per passage max. (2 hrs)
    Thu
    LG timed drill: 2 games in 17 minutes each. Review diagrams. Memorize game-type templates. (2 hrs)
    Fri
    LR: Parallel Reasoning + Parallel Flaw. These are time-sinks — practice skipping and returning. (2 hrs)
    Sat
    Full timed PrepTest. Aim for -0 on LG. Review with Blind Review. Track section performance. (4 hrs)
    Sun
    Error analysis + update weak-area list. Watch J.Y. Ping video explanations for all missed. (2 hrs)
    Phase 3 — Weeks 8–12: Speed + AccuracyTimed drilling
    Mon
    LG: 4 games in 35 minutes. If you miss the time goal, drill that specific game type. (2.5 hrs)
    Tue
    LR: Timed section from a released PT (35 min). Score it. Drill every wrong answer. (2 hrs)
    Wed
    RC: Full timed RC section. Focus on the hardest passage type for you. Map before reading Qs. (2 hrs)
    Thu
    Mixed weak-area drill: Pull 40 questions from your error log question types. Timed. (2.5 hrs)
    Fri
    Review + mental prep: Read through error log summary. Light review only. Rest your brain. (1 hr)
    Sat
    Full timed PrepTest under real conditions (no breaks except official). Target: 168+. (3.5 hrs)
    Sun
    Deep error review + Blind Review comparison. Speed vs understanding diagnosis. (2 hrs)
    Phase 4 — Weeks 13–16: Peak PerformanceTest simulation
    Mon
    Full timed PT 80–90 (recent tests). These most closely resemble the current LSAT format. (3.5 hrs)
    Tue
    Target your #1 weakest question type only. 30 targeted questions. (1.5 hrs)
    Wed
    Full timed PT. Scoring 170+ consistently? Your prep is working. If not: identify what section. (3.5 hrs)
    Thu
    LG warm-up: 4 games timed. LR: 20 questions from recent PTs. Light review only. (2 hrs)
    Fri
    Final week: Do NOT take a full PT. Light review of your strongest question types for confidence. (1 hr)
    Sat
    Rest day. Lay out materials. Sleep 8+ hours. Eat a real breakfast. Trust the work. (0 hrs)
    Sun
    Test day. Arrive early. Breathe. Flag hard questions and return. Your prep is done. Execute. 🎯
    🎯 The single most important 170+ habit
    Blind Review every single practice test. After your timed run, go back through every question with unlimited time. If your BR score is 172 but your timed score is 165, the problem is pacing — not understanding. If your BR is only 168, you still have conceptual gaps. Know which problem you have before deciding how to fix it.

    High School Roadmap to Law School
    A grade-by-grade plan for students who want a JD
    📌 Start here
    You don't need to decide on law school in 9th grade. But there are specific habits, courses, and experiences that make the path significantly easier — and some doors that close if you wait. This guide gives you a grade-by-grade action plan that keeps all options open.

    The pre-law skill stack you're building

    ✍️Writing — the #1 law skill
    • Lawyers write arguments for a living. Start now.
    • Take AP English Language (argument focus), not just Literature.
    • Write for your school newspaper or blog regularly.
    • Learn to write short, clear, direct sentences. Cut every word that doesn't earn its place.
    • Read legal briefs on CourtListener.com — see how lawyers actually argue.
    🎤Speaking & debate
    • Mock trial and moot court are the closest high school analogs to actual lawyering.
    • Lincoln-Douglas debate builds logical argument structure directly usable on the LSAT.
    • Model UN teaches international law concepts + diplomacy.
    • Join your school debate team — even one year dramatically improves LSAT scores.
    📖Reading critically
    • LSAT Reading Comprehension tests dense academic texts: science, law, humanities.
    • Read the New York Times editorial page, The Economist, academic journals.
    • Practice summarizing what you read in 2 sentences: main point + author's evidence.
    • Read Supreme Court opinions — start with Marbury v. Madison and Brown v. Board.
    🔢Logic & analytical thinking
    • LSAT Logical Reasoning = formal logic. AP Statistics, Calculus, and CS courses all help.
    • Logic puzzles, chess, programming — all build the pattern-recognition LG requires.
    • Philosophy and critical thinking courses are directly applicable.
    • Practice spotting flawed arguments in everyday media and political statements.
    ✅ The undergrad major question
    There is NO pre-law major. Law schools admit from every discipline. Political Science is most common (~18%) but not preferred. High GPA in a rigorous major matters more than the major itself. Philosophy, Economics, Math, and English are all excellent — they build the skills law school actually tests. STEM majors with high GPAs are increasingly attractive to law schools, especially for IP and tech law.

    Bar Exam Guide
    Pass rates, prep costs, UBE vs. state exams, and a timeline from 3L through swearing in
    📌 The basics
    The bar exam is a 2-day test taken after graduation. Day 1: the Multistate Bar Examination (MBE) — 200 multiple choice questions. Day 2: written essays and performance tests. Most states use the Uniform Bar Exam (UBE), which is portable — pass once, transfer your score to other UBE states for 2–5 years.
    🗺️UBE vs. Non-UBE States
    • UBE states (41+): NY, TX, IL, CO, WA, AZ, MN, and most others. One score, portable nationally.
    • Non-UBE states: CA, LA, FL (partially). Each has its own exam format.
    • California: Notoriously hardest bar. Separate 3-day exam. Pass rate ~40%.
    • Score portability: A 270+ UBE score transfers to NY, TX, CO, and most other UBE states.
    • Check your target state at ncbex.org
    📊Pass Rates by State (2024)
    • Wisconsin: ~97% (diploma privilege for in-state grads)
    • Missouri: ~87%
    • Montana / Iowa: ~82–85%
    • New York: ~77% overall; T14 grads ~92%
    • Texas: ~74% overall
    • Illinois: ~73%
    • Florida: ~68%
    • California: ~40% — lowest in the nation
    💰Bar Prep Costs
    • Barbri: $3,500–$4,200 (most comprehensive)
    • Themis: $1,695–$1,995 (solid, adaptive)
    • Kaplan: $1,999–$2,999
    • Adaptibar (MBE only): $299–$399
    • Bar exam registration: $300–$800 depending on state
    • MPRE (ethics exam): $125 — take during 2L/3L year
    • Character & fitness: $100–$400
    • Total budget: $4,500–$6,000 all-in
    📅Bar Exam Timeline
    • 2L year: Take the MPRE. Schedule early. Pass once, valid 3–5 years.
    • 3L fall: Register for the February or July bar in your target state.
    • 3L spring (Feb–May): Begin light review. Buy prep course early for discount.
    • Graduation (May): Full-time bar prep begins immediately. Treat it like a job.
    • July bar exam: 2 days, usually last Tuesday/Wednesday of July.
    • Results: Released October–November. BigLaw start dates are after results.
    • If you fail: Retake in February. Most BigLaw firms defer start date.
    📝MBE Subject Breakdown
    • Civil Procedure — 27 questions
    • Constitutional Law — 27 questions
    • Contracts — 27 questions
    • Criminal Law & Procedure — 27 questions
    • Evidence — 27 questions
    • Real Property — 27 questions
    • Torts — 27 questions
    • Total: 200 questions over 6 hours
    • Passing score: 133–144 scaled depending on state
    🎯How to Pass on the First Try
    • Start prep immediately after graduation — 10 weeks minimum
    • Do 50+ MBE practice questions every single day
    • Write at least 2 practice essays per day in weeks 6–10
    • Follow your prep course schedule — do not skip days
    • Sleep, eat, and exercise — cognitive stamina matters on day 2
    • Take 2 full simulated exams before test day
    • Know the rule statements cold — graders reward clear IRAC structure
    💡 PMBR / First-timers tip
    First-time takers at ABA-accredited schools pass at ~78% nationally. Students who follow their prep course schedule consistently pass at 85%+. The biggest predictor of failure is starting prep late or treating bar study like casual studying. Treat the 10 weeks after graduation as the most important professional test of your life — because it is.

    1L Survival Guide
    What to expect in your first year of law school — the honest version
    ⚠️ Reality check
    1L is genuinely hard — not because the material is impossible, but because the volume, the curve, the Socratic method, and the social pressure combine in ways nothing prepares you for. This guide is the honest version that current students wish they had before orientation.
    📚The Core 1L Curriculum
    • Contracts — offer, acceptance, consideration, breach, remedies. Abstract and logical.
    • Torts — negligence, intentional torts, strict liability. Most intuitive for new students.
    • Civil Procedure — how lawsuits actually work. Jurisdiction, pleadings, discovery. Dry but essential.
    • Constitutional Law — foundational. Heavy on case reading and political theory.
    • Criminal Law — actus reus, mens rea, defenses. Engaging but dense.
    • Property — land, estates, future interests. Hardest for most 1Ls. Start early.
    • Legal Research & Writing — how to write like a lawyer. Graded separately. Do not neglect it.
    🎤The Socratic Method
    • Professors cold-call students and ask probing questions about assigned cases. You do not get to say "I don't know."
    • Brief every case before class — facts, issue, holding, reasoning.
    • Professors are not trying to humiliate you — they are teaching you to think on your feet, which is what lawyers do.
    • Never lie about reading — professors can tell immediately. Just say you need more time to formulate your answer.
    • Everyone gets called on. Everyone stumbles. It gets easier by week 4.
    📊Grading & The Curve
    • Most schools have a mandatory median curve, usually B or B+.
    • In a class of 90, often only 10–15% get As. The rest get Bs.
    • One exam at the end of the semester = your entire grade for that course.
    • This means you can feel totally lost all semester and still do well on the exam if you master the material in the last 4 weeks.
    • Exam performance is a skill separate from class performance. Practice exams are non-negotiable.
    • Grade anxiety is universal. Grades come out in January. Breathe.
    📝How to Brief a Case
    • Facts: Who are the parties? What happened? What did the lower court decide?
    • Issue: The legal question the court must answer. One sentence.
    • Holding: What the court decided. One sentence.
    • Reasoning: Why the court decided that way. The most important part.
    • Rule: The legal principle that emerges from the decision.
    • Keep briefs short — 1 page max. You are reading for comprehension, not transcription.
    • After week 6, you will be able to brief cases in your head in 10 minutes.
    📓Outlines & Exam Prep
    • An outline is a condensed synthesis of the entire course organized by legal rule.
    • Start outlining around week 6–7. Do not wait until finals.
    • Use commercial outlines (Examples & Explanations, Emanuel) as a supplement only — your professor's outline is what matters for your exam.
    • Do old exams. Most professors post past exams. Doing 3 practice exams is worth more than 30 hours of extra reading.
    • Form a study group of 3–4 people — not more. Test each other.
    • IRAC: Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion. Every answer. Every time.
    🧠Mental Health & Balance
    • Law school has a well-documented mental health crisis. You are not weak for struggling.
    • Most schools have free counseling services specifically for law students — use them.
    • The students who seem most confident in class are often the most anxious at home.
    • Do not compare yourself to classmates constantly. The curve means someone always looks better.
    • Exercise, sleep, and eating are not luxuries — they are performance inputs.
    • If you are having thoughts of self-harm: 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988). You are not alone.
    🗓️1L Timeline
    • Aug–Sep: Orientation, case reading shock, first Socratic cold calls
    • Oct: OCI recruiting begins for some firms. 1L grades not out yet.
    • Nov: Begin outlining. Writing memo due at most schools.
    • Dec: Finals. Pure exam-taking mode. 2–3 weeks of exams.
    • Jan: 1L grades released. 1L recruiting for summer programs opens.
    • Feb–Mar: Apply for 1L summer jobs, public interest fellowships, firm programs.
    • Apr–May: Spring finals. Secure summer position.
    • Summer: 1L internship, research assistant, or public interest position.
    🚫1L Mistakes to Avoid
    • Skipping class — the Socratic method means absence is visible and costly
    • Not doing practice exams — the biggest GPA mistake 1Ls make
    • Over-briefing cases — 3 pages per case is a trap that wastes time
    • Waiting until week 12 to start outlining — too late
    • Isolating yourself — law school is a team sport socially
    • Neglecting the LRW course — it is graded and matters for journal selection
    • Giving up after first set of grades — many great lawyers had a rough 1L fall

    Networking & LinkedIn Guide for Law Students
    How to build a legal career network from scratch — even as a 0L
    👤LinkedIn Profile Essentials
    • Headline: "J.D. Candidate, [School] Law School '27 | Corporate Law | LSAT 170+" — not just "Law Student"
    • About section: 3–4 sentences on why law, what area, and what you bring. Write in first person.
    • Education: Include LSAT score if 165+ and GPA if 3.7+. List honors, journal, moot court.
    • Experience: Every internship, clinic, RA position, and relevant undergrad activity.
    • Skills: Add Legal Research, Westlaw, LexisNexis, Contract Drafting, Legal Writing.
    • Professional headshot is non-negotiable. LinkedIn profiles with photos get 21x more profile views.
    📧Cold Outreach That Works
    • Connect with attorneys at your target firms/agencies. Include a short personalized note — never the default "I'd like to connect."
    • Template: "Hi [Name], I am a 1L at [School] interested in [practice area]. Your work on [specific matter] caught my attention. Would you be open to a 15-minute informational call?"
    • Follow up once after 10 days if no response. Then stop.
    • Ask for 15 minutes, not 30 or 60. Respect their time.
    • Come prepared with 3–4 specific questions. Do not ask "what do you do?"
    • Send a thank-you email within 24 hours. Always.
    🏛️Formal Networking Channels
    • OCI (On-Campus Interviews): Firms come to your school. Highest-yield channel for BigLaw.
    • Law school career services: Use them aggressively — they have relationships you don't.
    • Bar association events: Most local bars have a Young Lawyers Division with free/cheap events.
    • Alumni network: Your law school alumni are required by tradition to help fellow alumni. Email them.
    • Clinics: Working alongside practicing attorneys is networking you get credit for.
    • Law review / journal: Fellow editors go everywhere — the network starts immediately.
    📅Networking by Year
    • 0L (before school): Connect with current students at target schools. Join BLSA, HLSA, or ACS LinkedIn groups.
    • 1L fall: Attend every firm reception. Collect cards. Follow up same evening.
    • 1L spring: Apply for 1L diversity programs at firms (Skadden, Orrick, Kirkland all have 1L programs).
    • 2L summer: Summer associate = a 10-week job interview. Be excellent and visible.
    • 3L: Stay warm with your summer firm contacts. Build the next layer of relationships through clinics and pro bono.

    How to Get a Federal Clerkship
    The most underrated career move in law — what it is, who gets it, and how to apply
    🏆 Why a federal clerkship changes everything
    A 1-year federal circuit clerkship typically comes with a $100,000–$150,000 signing bonus when you laterals to BigLaw afterward, in addition to your base salary. It provides unmatched exposure to appellate advocacy, writing, and judicial reasoning. Many top firms and academia regard it as the most prestigious credential outside of a T3 degree.
    🏛️Types of Federal Clerkships
    • U.S. Supreme Court: 3–4 clerks per Justice. Virtually requires T3 + circuit clerkship first. Paid ~$100K.
    • U.S. Circuit Courts (1st–11th, DC, Federal): Most prestigious tier. 2 clerks per judge. Paid ~$90K.
    • U.S. District Courts: Trial-level. More available. Great for litigation track. ~$85K.
    • Specialized courts: Court of Federal Claims, Tax Court, Bankruptcy Court — great for niche practice areas.
    • State Supreme Courts: Excellent but not "federal." Still very prestigious.
    📊Who Gets Circuit Clerkships?
    • T14 law school: near-requirement for circuit; helpful but not required for district
    • Top 10–15% of class (law review or equivalent journal)
    • Strong legal writing sample — typically a seminar paper or law review note
    • Strong professor recommendation from someone the judge knows personally
    • Ideally: moot court participation, federal or appellate litigation exposure
    • District clerkships: more accessible. T25 with top grades and writing skill is competitive.
    📅Application Timeline (Critical)
    • 1L spring/summer: Research judges. Start identifying targets based on location and ideology.
    • 1L summer: Ask professors for recommendations NOW — before grades come out if possible.
    • 2L fall (September): OSCAR applications open. Submit applications immediately — many judges review in September.
    • 2L fall (Oct–Dec): Most offers made. Some judges hire very early, some very late.
    • 2L spring: Mop-up — some judges still hiring for the following year.
    • 3L year: Some judges hire on a 1-year cycle instead of 2-year. Keep watching OSCAR.
    📝What to Submit (OSCAR)
    • Cover letter: 1 page. Why this judge, why this court, why you. Personalize every letter.
    • Resume: 1 page. Clean, conservative format. Judges are old school.
    • Transcripts: Unofficial is fine for applications. Official sent if invited.
    • Writing sample: 10–20 pages. Best legal writing you have. Law review note ideal. No co-authored pieces.
    • 3 recommendations: Professors who know your writing and thinking deeply. Not just "she was in my class."
    • Apply to 50–150 judges across your target circuits. Volume matters.

    🏢BigLaw Reality Check
    • Only ~15% of law grads join BigLaw. Most came from T14 schools.
    • ~80% of BigLaw associates leave by year 5 — voluntarily or otherwise.
    • The up-or-out model is real. Partnership is genuinely competitive.
    • The $225K buys your time — expect 2,000–2,400 billable hours per year.
    • Golden handcuffs are real. Lifestyle inflation on a BigLaw salary makes it hard to leave.
    ⚡Boutique Advantages
    • Direct client contact from day 1 — you are not just document review
    • Specialty boutiques (IP, antitrust, insurance) often rival BigLaw in hourly rates
    • Better mentorship — fewer associates per partner
    • More predictable hours in many practices
    • Mid-range salary is genuinely hard with heavy law school debt — run the numbers first
    🏛️Government Underrated Perks
    • PSLF forgives ALL remaining federal loans after 10 years — worth $200K+ for heavy borrowers
    • FEHB health insurance — best employer-sponsored healthcare in the country
    • Federal Employees Retirement System (FERS) pension — virtually extinct in private sector
    • Trial experience: federal prosecutors try cases in year 1. BigLaw associates may not see a trial for 7 years.
    • Work that matters: prosecuting cartels, defending civil rights, shaping policy

    Law Review & Moot Court Explained
    What they are, why they matter, and how to get on them
    📰What is Law Review?
    • Law review is a student-run academic journal that publishes legal scholarship.
    • Every T14 school and most T50 schools have a flagship "Law Review" plus 10–20 specialty journals (environmental law, international law, etc.).
    • The flagship law review is the most prestigious. Specialty journals are still excellent and more accessible.
    • Members edit articles submitted by law professors and write their own student "notes" — typically 30–60 pages of original legal analysis.
    • Law review membership signals research ability, writing skill, and commitment — exactly what judges, firms, and academia want to see.
    🚪How to Get on Law Review
    • Grades-on: Top 10–15% of 1L class automatically qualifies. Most important path.
    • Write-on competition: All students can submit during 1L spring. You are given a packet of sources and must write a short note or edit a sample in 3–5 days.
    • Combined: Many schools use a weighted combination of grades and write-on score.
    • Prepare for the write-on by practicing the Bluebook and editing short samples throughout 1L spring.
    • If you do not make flagship, apply to specialty journals — they often take a broader range of students and are still excellent resume entries.
    🎤What is Moot Court?
    • Moot court is a simulated appellate argument competition where students argue both sides of a legal issue before a panel of judges (often real attorneys or actual judges).
    • You submit a written brief and then argue orally — judges interrupt with questions constantly.
    • It is the most direct simulation of actual appellate lawyering that exists in law school.
    • Most schools have an internal competition in 1L spring. Top performers are invited to join the moot court board and represent the school in national competitions.
    • National competitions include the National Moot Court Competition, Jessup International, and dozens of specialty competitions.
    🏆Why Both Matter for Your Career
    • Federal clerkships: Virtually required to have law review or equivalent journal. A strong writing sample from your student note is often your best asset.
    • BigLaw litigation: Moot court participation signals oral advocacy ability — firms notice this in interviews.
    • Academia: Law review is essentially mandatory. Your student note can become your first publication.
    • Public interest: Both signal commitment and capability. Less gatekept here but still valued.
    • The real benefit: Writing and editing under deadline pressure with expert feedback makes you a dramatically better lawyer regardless of career path.
    ⏱️Time Commitment
    • Law review (staff): 8–12 hours/week during 2L year. Editing, cite-checking, writing your note.
    • Law review (editor position, 3L): 15–25 hours/week. Managing submissions, editing students.
    • Moot court (team member): 10–20 hours/week during competition season (usually fall 2L).
    • Both are time-consuming. Do not join both flagship law review and the moot court team simultaneously unless your grades are very strong and you have no other major commitments.
    • Choose based on your career path: litigation = moot court; clerkship/academia = law review.
    📘Other Extracurriculars Worth Joining
    • Transactional moot court (negotiation competitions): Great for corporate/M&A track
    • Trial advocacy teams: ATLA competition, National Trial Competition — litigation track
    • Legal clinics: Real clients, real cases, real court appearances. Best experiential learning.
    • Student Bar Association (SBA): Leadership role builds administrative skills and visibility
    • Pro bono work: Many schools require 50 hours. Go beyond the minimum — it is real legal work.
