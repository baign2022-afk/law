    Law School Scholarship Guide
    How to fund your legal education in the USA
    📋Types of scholarships
    • Merit scholarships — awarded based on LSAT + GPA. Most impactful lever.
    • Need-based grants — FAFSA-determined; varies by school.
    • Diversity scholarships — race, gender, first-gen, LGBTQ+, veterans.
    • Public interest fellowships — LRAP programs that forgive loans for public service jobs.
    • External scholarships — private orgs, bar foundations, employers.
    • State bar scholarships — most state bars fund 1–3 students per year.
    🎯The LSAT leverage strategy
    • Every 3-point LSAT increase can unlock $15,000–$40,000/yr more in merit aid.
    • Apply to schools where your LSAT is above their 75th percentile — you become a "splitter" they want to recruit.
    • A 170+ opens full-ride offers at T14–T25 schools regularly.
    • Retake if below your target school median — scholarship money follows the score.
    • Use 7Sage scholarship estimator to model your package before applying.
    💰Top external scholarships
    • LSAC Diversity Scholarship — up to $15,000 for underrepresented students.
    • Paul & Daisy Soros Fellowship — $90,000 for New Americans in any graduate program.
    • ABA Legal Opportunity Scholarship — $15,000 for diverse 1Ls.
    • Hispanic National Bar Foundation — multiple awards for Latinx students.
    • National Black Law Students Assoc. — regional and national awards.
    • Women's Bar Association — state chapters fund $1,000–$10,000.
    📬Negotiating your offer
    • Always negotiate. 70%+ of schools will increase offers if asked professionally.
    • Use competing offers as leverage — name the school and the amount.
    • Write a 1-page letter: express genuine interest, state the competing offer, ask for a match.
    • Call your admissions contact — a 10-minute call is more effective than email.
    • Negotiate after deposit deadline passes — waitlisted spots free up scholarship funds.
    • Ask about "scholarship retention" requirements — GPA thresholds that can cost you aid.
    ⚖️Loan Repayment Assistance (LRAP)
    • If you plan a public service career, LRAP can be worth more than a scholarship.
    • Yale, Harvard, Stanford, Columbia all offer generous LRAP covering loans fully for public interest jobs.
    • Federal PSLF forgives remaining balance after 10 years of qualifying payments.
    • IBR/PAYE plans cap monthly payments at 10% of discretionary income.
    • Apply for LRAP in your 3L year — most programs require annual renewal.
    📅Application timeline
    June–Aug: Take the LSAT
    Aim for your target score before October. Retakes in Oct/Nov are fine.
    Sep–Oct: Research schools
    Build your list: 3 reach, 4 target, 3 safety schools. Check median LSAT for each.
    Oct–Nov: Submit applications
    Apply early — rolling admissions means first-come, first-served for scholarships.
    Jan–Mar: Scholarship offers arrive
    Compare packages. Begin negotiation letters.
    Apr 15: Decision deadline
    Deposit deadline. Use competing offers to negotiate up until this date.
    💡 Pro tip: The "scholarship splitter" strategy
    Apply to schools where your LSAT is 5+ points above their median. Schools ranked #15–#50 regularly offer full-tuition scholarships to attract students with high LSAT scores, even when those students have lower GPAs. A 172 with a 3.4 GPA might get rejected from Yale but offered a full ride at Emory, Wake Forest, or Notre Dame. Run the math — a full ride at a T25 school often beats $50K/yr debt at a T5.

    Scoring 170+ on the LSAT
    What the 97th+ percentile actually takes — and a 16-week study plan

    Understanding the score scale

    120–149Below 40th pct.
    Below most law school medians. A retake with structured prep is strongly recommended.
    150–15940th–70th pct.
    Competitive for T3 and regional schools. Scholarships limited. Strong GPA can offset.
    160–164~80th pct.
    Strong. Competitive for T1 schools outside T14. Merit scholarships start here.
    165–169~90th–96th pct.
    Excellent. Competitive at T14. Strong merit scholarship territory at T15–T50.
    170–17497th–99th pct.
    Full scholarship territory at T14. Competitive at Yale/Harvard/Stanford. Transformative for your options.
    175–18099th+ pct.
    Rare. ~500 people per year. Virtually guarantees admission and scholarship at any US law school.

    What separates 170+ scorers

    🧠Mindset shift
    • Don't memorize tricks — understand the reasoning structure behind every question type.
    • Every wrong answer has a specific, identifiable flaw. Find it, don't skip it.
    • Drill your weaknesses, not your strengths. Comfort ≠ improvement.
    • Aim for -0 or -1 on Logic Games. LG is the most learnable section.
    • Read every answer choice — even when you "know" the answer.
    📚Best resources for 170+
    • LSAT Demon — AI-adaptive, built specifically for 170+ chasers. No tricks, all understanding.
    • 7Sage + J.Y. Ping explanations — video explanations for every LSAC question.
    • LSAC LawHub — 70+ released PrepTests. Do every one under timed conditions.
    • PowerScore Logic Games Bible — master diagramming before anything else.
    • Reddit r/LSAT — community debrief of recent tests, tips from 17X scorers.
    • Blind Review method — take tests twice: timed, then unlimited. Gap = your ceiling.
    ⏱️Time management on test day
    • LR: 1 min 25 sec per question average. Skip and return — don't sink on one question.
    • LG: Spend 1–2 min diagramming each game before solving. It saves time overall.
    • RC: Read for structure, not details. Mark where the author argues, concedes, and concludes.
    • Flag and return: mark any question taking 90+ seconds. Come back if time allows.
    • Never leave blanks — guess if needed. No penalty for wrong answers.
    📊Tracking your progress
    • Keep an error log for every wrong answer: question type, why you got it wrong, correct reasoning.
    • Do a "PT analysis" after each timed test: categorize errors by question type.
    • Track your "Blind Review score" — what you get with unlimited time. If BR - timed score > 5, your issue is speed not understanding.
    • Don't test too frequently. One PT per week max in early prep, 2/week in final month.

    16-Week 170+ Study Plan

    Click each phase to expand the weekly plan. Target: 15–20 hours/week. Starting score assumed: 155–162.

    Phase 1 — Weeks 1–3: FoundationsCore concepts
    Mon
    Read PowerScore Logic Games Bible Ch. 1–3. Complete 2 introductory LG games with diagram practice. (2.5 hrs)
    Tue
    Logical Reasoning: Study "Must Be True" and "Main Point" question types via 7Sage or LSAT Demon. Do 20 untimed LR questions. (2 hrs)
    Wed
    Reading Comprehension: Read 2 RC passages, practice identifying structure: main point, author's tone, evidence vs. claim. (1.5 hrs)
    Thu
    LG drill: 3 games from PrepTests 1–10 untimed. Focus on diagramming accuracy, not speed. Review every error. (2.5 hrs)
    Fri
    LR: Study "Flaw" and "Weaken" questions. Do 25 questions. Build your error log. (2 hrs)
    Sat
    First full untimed PrepTest (PT 30–35). Blind Review afterward — mark every uncertain answer. (4 hrs)
    Sun
    Error review: For every wrong answer, write out WHY the correct answer is correct. (1.5 hrs)
    Phase 2 — Weeks 4–7: Section MasteryDeep drilling
    Mon
    LG: Complete all game types (sequencing, grouping, in/out, combo). 4 games untimed, then retimed. (2.5 hrs)
    Tue
    LR: Deep dive on Strengthen/Assumption/Sufficient Assumption. These 3 types make up 35%+ of LR. Do 30 questions. (2.5 hrs)
    Wed
    RC: Comparative reading passages. Practice passage mapping. Time yourself: 3.5 min per passage max. (2 hrs)
    Thu
    LG timed drill: 2 games in 17 minutes each. Review diagrams. Start memorizing game-type templates. (2 hrs)
    Fri
    LR: Parallel Reasoning + Parallel Flaw. These are time-sinks — practice skipping and returning. (2 hrs)
    Sat
    Full timed PrepTest. Aim for -0 on LG. Review with Blind Review method. Track section-by-section performance. (4 hrs)
    Sun
    Error analysis + update your weak-area list. Watch J.Y. Ping video explanations for all missed questions. (2 hrs)
    Phase 3 — Weeks 8–12: Speed + AccuracyTimed drilling
    Mon
    LG: 4 games in 35 minutes. If you miss the time goal, drill the specific game type that slowed you. (2.5 hrs)
    Tue
    LR: Timed section from a released PT (35 min). Score it. Drill every wrong answer. (2 hrs)
    Wed
    RC: Full timed RC section. Focus on the hardest passage type for you. Map before reading questions. (2 hrs)
    Thu
    Mixed weak-area drill: Pull 40 questions from your error log question types. Timed. (2.5 hrs)
    Fri
    Review + mental prep: Read through your error log summary. Light review only. Rest your brain. (1 hr)
    Sat
    Full timed PrepTest under real conditions (no breaks except official). Target: 168+. (3.5 hrs)
    Sun
    Deep error review + Blind Review comparison. If BR score is 170+ but timed is 164, focus on speed this week. (2 hrs)
    Phase 4 — Weeks 13–16: Peak PerformanceTest simulation
    Mon
    Full timed PT 80–90 (recent tests). These most closely resemble the current LSAT format. (3.5 hrs)
    Tue
    Target your #1 weakest question type only. 30 targeted questions. (1.5 hrs)
    Wed
    Full timed PT. If scoring 170+ consistently: your prep is working. If not: identify the specific section dragging you down. (3.5 hrs)
    Thu
    LG warm-up: 4 games timed. LR: 20 questions from recent PTs. Light review only. (2 hrs)
    Fri
    Final week before test: Do NOT take a full PT. Light review of your strongest question types for confidence. (1 hr)
    Sat
    Rest day. Lay out your materials. Sleep 8+ hours. Eat a real breakfast. You have prepared — trust the work. (0 hrs)
    Sun
    Test day. Arrive early. Breathe. Flag hard questions and return. Your prep is done. Execute. 🎯
    🎯 The single most important 170+ habit
    Blind Review every single practice test. After your timed run, go back through every question with unlimited time. The gap between your BR score and your timed score is your target. If your BR is already 172 but your timed score is 165, the problem is pacing — not understanding. If your BR is only 168, you still have conceptual gaps to fill. Know which problem you have before deciding how to fix it.
